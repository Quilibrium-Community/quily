# dev-updates - July 20, 2026

## Cassie — 5:34 AM UTC

.25 branch update (not version release) is happening within the next 24 hours, including migration steps required of archives. I am presently cleaning things up from testing and ensuring common node deployment configurations will properly adapt to the changes in this branch. For those testing or building against node spec, there are some very important details. After exhaustive performance analysis, there were key gaps identified, we addressed each in different ways, but culminating in a common motif: We have eliminated all non-post quantum safe intrinsics from use. The release of .25 will mean there will be no later stage of a PQC migration, we'll be ready immediately.

## Cassie — 5:34 AM UTC

It is critical to state the reason for this is a consequence of the performance analysis – being PQ-ready was a pleasant side effect in a search for a solution to particular issues. To elaborate:
- **BLS48-581 (Signatures) -> Falcon** – After a number of different iterations of optimization via aggregation, even alternative curve representations, the net effect was that there was no sufficient way, short of a requirement of specialized server configurations and pre-arranged networking agreements, that this would remain stable in larger shard counts. While Falcon does not have an aggregation scheme, the speed dramatically outperformed BLS48-581 in every meaningful metric for every foreseeable aggregation concern.
- **ED448 (Peer keys -> Falcon), (mTLS -> NTRU)** – Same situation applied here. We utilize peer keys not only for p2p message attestation, but also in mutual authentication. This mutual auth validation scheme ultimately was expensive, but tractible in small peer counts. What we were finding was every time the network was beginning to come back to life, opportunistic miners who waited until such a trend emerged, would spin up more nodes, ultimately clotheslining all threads in managing the validation. We attempted limited thread pooling, but this became a bottleneck for peers needing to sync or submit requests, then attempted additional caching, but the new peers spinning up would have new keys making the cache moot. Switching to Falcon in testing was immediately higher performance for signature verification, and when swapping our ed448 noise transport to NTRU, it collapsed the verification time and processor burden similarly.

## Cassie — 5:34 AM UTC

- **Verkle/KZG -> Merkle** - Numbers are several orders of magnitude in non AVX512 cases. We're talking a jump from low seconds to low milliseconds in worst case.
- **Decaf448/Schnorr/Bulletproofs (Tokens, E2EE, some MPC construction) -> BDLOP/M-SIS/M-LWE** - The same mathematical trick behind monero's confidential commitments, zk range/sum proofs that we used in decaf448 translated without issue to the PQC realm, and the speedup was similarly quite good. The addition of SIS-accumulator proofs reduced overhead of privacy preservation and allowed us to kill more slow code.
- **Removing hand-rolled generic consensus logic** - Outside of the PQC realm, we have also stripped out hand-rolled implementations of libraries we had to manually implement in go, resulting in a near complete removal of all ported generic consensus library logic, adopting battle tested libraries. By consequence, the overall time-to-confirmation average in testing reduced from a 1-3s range to sub-second.

## Cassie — 5:34 AM UTC

There are a lot of huge impacts that this work has resulted in, and this list is not exhaustive. Overall, where we were seeing strain most from the highly instrumented archives, led to these concrete solutions. It was, essentially, a requirement to choose algorithms that maintained _at least_ the level of security parameters chosen, and we were pleasantly surprised to see that the PQC research field has dramatically matured and leveled up in the past year, especially with respect to performance.

This will also be a great demonstration of a core design decision from the very beginning: our support of cryptographic agility – being able to swap out primitives that share purpose is something we detailed as a requirement, not just because of the emerging threat of quantum computers, but also due to the possibility that established cryptosystems themselves can be broken in surprising ways. But this isn't a moment for publishing such an attack, this is a moment for ensuring it does not concern us.
